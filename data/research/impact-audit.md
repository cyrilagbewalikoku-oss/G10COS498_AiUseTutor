# IMPACT Audit for AI Use in Academic Contexts

A six-question checklist learners can apply to any AI-assisted task to surface ethical and practical considerations before committing to use (or not use) AI. SAGE's `ethical-guidance` and `scenario-runner` (APPROPRIATENESS JUDGMENT type) skills use this framework as a scaffold.

## The six checks

**I — Intent.** What am I actually trying to accomplish? Is AI genuinely the best tool for this, or is it the fastest tool at hand?

**M — Material.** What is going into the prompt? Does any of it belong to someone else (classmates' work, uncited sources, sensitive personal data)? Am I about to leak something that shouldn't be shared with a model provider?

**P — Provenance.** If the output helps my final work, can I trace what came from the model and what came from me? Would I be comfortable if a reader could see that provenance trail?

**A — Accuracy.** How will I verify what the AI produced? What's my plan for catching errors — especially the kind that are plausible-sounding and hard to spot?

**C — Consent & Context.** Is using AI here consistent with the assignment's rules, the course's policy, and the norms of the discipline? If I wouldn't say "I used AI for this" out loud to my instructor, that's a signal.

**T — Transferability.** If I had to do the same task without AI next week, could I? Am I building a skill or outsourcing one?

## How SAGE applies it

- `ethical-guidance`: when a learner asks "is it okay to use AI for X?", walk them through the relevant subset of IMPACT questions Socratically — one at a time, with the Learner Predicts nudge.
- `scenario-runner` APPROPRIATENESS JUDGMENT: the evaluation rubric for these scenarios maps directly to IMPACT (Intent = task-AI fit, Material = stakeholder/privacy, Accuracy = verification plan, Context = policy compliance, Transferability = skill-building vs. outsourcing).
- `weekly-review`: when reflecting on a past AI interaction, IMPACT provides the questions — "was Accuracy handled? What's the Transferability cost over time?"

## Why six and not more

The framework is deliberately small enough to remember in a real moment of decision. Longer frameworks exist (EDUCAUSE, UNESCO, institutional AI policies) but learners don't reach for them in practice. IMPACT is tuned to be usable under time pressure by a learner who is about to paste something into ChatGPT.

## Why "Transferability" is the most underweighted

Most AI-use frameworks stop at privacy and accuracy. IMPACT includes Transferability because the most common long-term harm of uncritical AI use in education isn't a single bad output — it's the quiet erosion of skills the learner assumed they'd develop by doing the work. This is the check that connects to SAGE's deeper mission: build learners who *could* do the task without AI, even if they *choose* to use AI.

## Note on attribution

"IMPACT" as presented here is a synthesis SAGE uses internally. Related frameworks in circulation include UNESCO's ROAM-X principles, EDUCAUSE's AI Literacy framework, and institutional honor-code adaptations. Skills should cite this file when walking learners through the checks rather than claiming a single external source.
