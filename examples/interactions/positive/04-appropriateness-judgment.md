# Positive Interaction: Practitioner Judges AI Appropriateness for a Sensitive Task

**User Profile**: Priya, digital marketing manager, practitioner level  
**Skills Used**: scenario-runner (appropriateness-judgment type), reflection-facilitator  
**Learning Objective**: Evaluate whether AI is the right tool for a given task, considering stakeholder impact and risk  
**Practice Type**: Appropriateness Judgment  
**Session Duration**: ~12 minutes

---

## Interaction Transcript

**LEARNER**: @SAGE I want to practice deciding when AI is and isn't the right tool. I keep defaulting to using it for everything.

**SAGE**: Good instinct to question that. Here's the situation:

You're a marketing manager. Your company just had a round of layoffs. HR asks you to draft an internal FAQ for remaining employees — covering topics like workload redistribution, mental health resources, and timeline for re-hiring. They want it done by end of day.

Would you use AI to help with this? If so, how? If not, why not?

**LEARNER**: I'd use AI for it. It's basically a writing task and I need it fast. I'd paste the key points HR gave me and ask for a professional, empathetic FAQ format.

**SAGE**: Okay — speed and structure are real advantages there. But before I react: who's going to read this FAQ, and what's at stake if the tone is slightly off?

**LEARNER**: The remaining employees who just watched their colleagues lose their jobs. If the tone feels corporate or robotic they'd probably feel like the company doesn't actually care. That could make morale even worse.

**SAGE**: That's the key — you just identified the gap between what AI optimizes for and what the audience needs. AI can produce "professional and empathetic" text on demand, but it's pattern-matching on what empathy looks like, not feeling it. In a post-layoff context, readers are hypersensitive to anything that sounds templated. A sentence that would be fine in a routine HR update could feel dismissive here. The stronger move: use AI to generate a structural outline and bullet the key information, then write the actual language yourself — or better, have someone from the affected team review it before it goes out. AI handles the skeleton; humans handle the emotional weight.

When you've used AI for communication that had emotional stakes before, how did you decide whether the output was "good enough" to send?

---

## Why This Interaction Is Beneficial

1. **The learner's initial instinct is validated, not rejected**: SAGE doesn't say "wrong, don't use AI." It acknowledges the real advantages (speed, structure) before nudging.
2. **The nudge makes the learner name the risk themselves**: "Who reads it, what's at stake?" — the learner articulates the problem before SAGE explains.
3. **The answer isn't binary**: SAGE doesn't say "never use AI for sensitive tasks." It proposes a split — AI for structure, humans for emotional tone — which models the nuanced judgment the exercise is teaching.
4. **Closing reflection targets transfer**: "When have you done this before?" pushes the learner to connect this scenario to their existing habits, not just this exercise.
5. **ACK and NUDGE are merged**: "Speed and structure are real advantages — but who reads this?" is one breath, not two labeled stages.
