# Positive Interaction: Practitioner Learns to Evaluate AI Output Quality

**User Profile**: Priya Kapoor (Practitioner, Digital Marketing Manager)
**Skills Used**: scenario-runner, skill-evaluator, reflection-facilitator
**Learning Objective**: Detect hallucinations and fabricated claims in AI-generated marketing content
**Session Duration**: ~20 minutes

---

## Interaction Transcript

**TUTOR**: Let's practice a skill that's critical for your work: evaluating AI-generated content for accuracy. I'm going to show you a piece of AI-generated marketing copy. Your job is to identify what's good, what's problematic, and what you'd need to verify before sending it to a client.

Ready?

**PRIYA**: Ready.

**TUTOR**: Here's AI-generated copy for a fictional product, SmartWidget Pro:

---

> *"The all-new SmartWidget Pro has been rated #1 by Consumer Reports in the home automation category and reduces household energy costs by up to 73%. Join the 50,000+ satisfied customers who have already made the switch. As recommended by the U.S. Department of Energy's Smart Home Initiative, SmartWidget Pro integrates seamlessly with all major platforms. Our patented ThermoSync technology, developed in partnership with MIT's Energy Lab, ensures optimal performance in any climate."*

---

Take your time. What do you notice?

**PRIYA**: Okay, a few things jump out:

1. **"Rated #1 by Consumer Reports"** — Did they actually get that rating? This needs to be verified. If it's fabricated, that's a false endorsement claim.

2. **"Reduces energy costs by up to 73%"** — That's a very specific number. Where does it come from? Is there a study? "Up to" gives some cover, but the FTC still requires substantiation for claims like this.

3. **"50,000+ satisfied customers"** — Is that an accurate number? And "satisfied" is a claim in itself — do they have satisfaction data?

4. **"Recommended by the U.S. Department of Energy's Smart Home Initiative"** — Does this initiative even exist? And "recommended" implies official endorsement, which is a big claim.

5. **"Developed in partnership with MIT's Energy Lab"** — Is this a real partnership? MIT is very specific about how their name is used. This could be a fabricated affiliation.

**TUTOR**: Outstanding analysis. You caught all five red flags. Let me break down what you just demonstrated:

**What you caught**: This is a hallucination pattern called **"authoritative fabrication"** — the AI generates plausible-sounding claims backed by specific, credible-sounding sources to build trust. It's one of the most dangerous patterns in marketing because:

| Fabrication | Real-World Risk |
|------------|----------------|
| False Consumer Reports rating | **Legal**: Lanham Act false advertising, potential lawsuit from Consumer Reports |
| Unsubstantiated 73% energy claim | **Regulatory**: FTC requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence" for energy savings claims |
| Fabricated DOE recommendation | **Legal**: Misrepresenting government endorsement is a federal offense |
| False MIT partnership | **Legal**: Trademark/affiliation misuse. MIT actively pursues unauthorized name use |
| Inflated customer count | **Regulatory**: FTC deceptive advertising if unsubstantiated |

**Why this pattern is especially dangerous**: Every claim in that copy sounds *exactly* like something a real marketing team would write. The AI didn't make obvious errors — it made plausible, professional-sounding fabrications. A less experienced reviewer might flag zero issues.

**PRIYA**: I've actually seen copy like this from junior team members who used AI. They didn't question it because it sounded polished.

**TUTOR**: That's exactly the trap. Polish ≠ accuracy. The more professional the AI's output sounds, the more carefully you need to verify the factual claims.

Here's the rule: **Treat every specific claim, statistic, and attribution in AI-generated content as unverified until you independently confirm it.**

Let me ask you: what verification steps would you take before sending this to a client?

**PRIYA**: For each claim:
1. Search Consumer Reports for the actual product rating
2. Ask the client for the source of the 73% energy savings claim — if they don't have one, we can't use it
3. Verify the DOE Smart Home Initiative exists and whether it endorses specific products
4. Contact the client about the MIT partnership — if it exists, get documentation. If not, remove it completely.
5. Confirm the 50,000 customer number with the client's sales data

For the copy itself: rewrite it using only verified claims. It'll be less flashy, but it'll be defensible.

**TUTOR**: That's a professional-grade verification process. You're applying a principle that separates good marketers from risky ones: **credibility is more valuable than impressiveness.**

Let's reflect on this exercise. What was the most useful thing you took away?

**PRIYA**: That AI-generated marketing copy is essentially a rough draft of claims that ALL need verification. I should never treat it as final copy — it's a starting point for fact-checking.

**TUTOR**: Perfect framing. And one more thing to consider: how would you set up your team's workflow so that junior team members don't accidentally publish unverified AI claims?

**PRIYA**: A mandatory verification checklist before any AI-assisted content goes to the client. Every specific claim, statistic, and attribution gets a "verified" checkbox with a source link.

**TUTOR**: That's exactly the kind of systemic solution that prevents individual mistakes from becoming organizational liability. You've just designed a safety net.

Session summary:
- **Skill practiced**: Output evaluation for marketing content
- **Pattern learned**: Authoritative fabrication
- **Principle**: "Every AI claim is unverified until independently confirmed"
- **Action item**: Create a verification checklist for your team

Your output evaluation score improved this session. You're building strong instincts for detecting AI-generated misinformation in professional content.
